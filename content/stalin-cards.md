# Section: Modernity and the Tsarist trap
id: modernity-and-tsarism

## Q: What is Kotkin's headline claim about what produced modernity — and what does he say was NOT the cause?

### A:

Modernity (modern steel, mass-based militaries, mass politics) was a response to vicious geopolitical competition: states either matched the other great powers or were crushed and potentially colonized.

It was not an inherent sociological process — not just "moving out of tradition."

---

## Q: Imperial Russia faced what Sarah Paine calls the continental power trap. State the trap, and the rate at which Imperial Russia expanded.

### A:

You have to conquer more territory to defend the territory you already have, because your long land borders are permeable to invasion.

Imperial Russia expanded ~50,000 km² per year for 450+ years — about the size of Slovakia or Costa Rica every single year.

---

## Q: Stolypin pursued land reform and further industrialization in late Tsarist Russia. By how much did Russian median incomes rise in the 20 years before the Bolshevik revolution, and why didn't this save the regime?

### A:

Median incomes rose ~50%.

But the new government had the support of nobody: liberals/constitutionalists thought it was too little, too late, and illegitimate; aristocrats thought reforms were against their interests. No constituency felt bought-in.

---

## Q: Solzhenitsyn calls Tsarism "weak and irresolute in pursuit of its enemies." What is the surprising fact about Lenin's biography he highlights?

### A:

In spring 1887, Lenin's brother was executed for an attempted assassination of Alexander III. That same autumn Lenin was admitted to the Imperial University at Kazan — to the Law Faculty.

Later, after founding the League of Struggle for the Liberation of the Working Class, Lenin was banished for three years to Minusinsk on a state stipend "more than he needed."

# Section: Succession, terror, and the divide-and-conquer playbook
id: succession-and-terror

## Q: Kotkin asks what Trotsky could have done in 1924 to safeguard the revolution against Stalin. What does he say Trotsky should have done?

### A:

Violated party discipline by reading aloud on Red Square from Lenin's purported dictation — using as his mantra Lenin's summons to "remove Stalin" as General Secretary — then flying from factory to factory to rally workers, just as in 1917, and letting them arrest him.

---

## Q: What lesson does Dwarkesh draw from comparing the failed OpenAI board attempt to remove Sam Altman ("not consistently candid") with the Old Bolsheviks who didn't go all-in against Stalin?

### A: No half measures. If you come for the king, you best not miss.

---

## Q: What was Stalin's playbook for purging rivals — the one Victor Shih says is also Xi's?

### A:

Align the main members of the party against the next person you want to purge. Rinse and repeat.

Stalin aligned Kamenev and Zinoviev against Trotsky, then Bukharin against Kamenev and Zinoviev — by the time it was Bukharin's turn, Stalin was already god-dictator.

---

## Q: Why didn't Old Bolsheviks like Bukharin and Zinoviev — hardened revolutionaries who had faced down the Tsar — use the Moscow show trials to denounce Stalin, given that execution was already certain?

### A:

Kotkin treats this as a real puzzle. Dwarkesh poses it directly: men who had overthrown a regime that lasted hundreds of years confessed to fabricated charges instead. The same pattern shows up with Liu Shaoqi under Mao.

The implicit answer is that the strategy of aligning the majority against the next target leaves no coordination point for the survivors — by the time you're a "dead man walking in your normal position of power," there is no audience left willing to act with you.

# Section: NEP, collectivization, and what they were for
id: nep-and-collectivization

## Q: What was Bukharin's slogan to peasants during the NEP, and what was the core ideological dilemma he was managing?

### A:

"Enrich yourselves, accumulate, develop your farms."

The dilemma: seven years into the NEP, socialism was not in sight. NEP amounted to grudgingly tolerated capitalism in a country that had had an avowedly anticapitalist revolution.

---

## Q: A "morbid contrarian" view holds that collectivization was a brutal-but-necessary speedrun to industrialization. What is Dwarkesh's (and Kotkin's implicit) reframe?

### A:

Collectivization actually reduced economic output and even harmed industrialization. The first five-year plan cut grain production by ~32% and halved livestock.

Its real function was to let the state control the full economy: production fell, but the state now controlled what grain remained, which it exported to fund military-industrial goals. Not a costly speedrun — a deliberate Pareto-frontier-collapsing transfer of control.

---

## Q: When did Soviet Russia's industrial output recover to its 1913 level, and what immediately happened after that recovery?

### A: In 1928. Then Stalin launched the first five-year plan, which reduced grain production by over 32% and led to the halving of overall livestock.

# Section: The lesser of two evils
id: lesser-of-two-evils

## Q: Dwarkesh poses a tension about supporting "the lesser of two evils" using Russia and Germany. State the tension.

### A:

The Russian examples support the rule: liberals should have backed the authoritarian-but-reformist Stolypin, and Provisional Government factions should have united to prevent the greater evil of the Bolsheviks.

The German example contradicts it: conservatives under Franz von Papen did support what they perceived as the lesser evil — Hitler — to stop the Communists, with catastrophic results.

# Section: Stalin's style
id: stalins-style

## Q: Stalin marked up countless memos with two recurring rhetorical formulas. State both, and what each signaled.

### A:

- Regarding something he thought must be changed: "Is it possible that we can allow X? Of course it is not."
- Regarding a supposed blocker to his will: "Is it possible to do X? Of course it is."

---

## Q: Why does Dwarkesh think Stalin was much more successful at foreign policy than domestic policy?

### A:

He was less ideological abroad than at home: he was reluctant to promote socialist or communist parties abroad (half-hearted support of communists in the Spanish Civil War, pushing Mao to stop at the Yangtze River instead of pursuing Chiang Kai-Shek). His domestic policy was the ideological one — and the destructive one.
